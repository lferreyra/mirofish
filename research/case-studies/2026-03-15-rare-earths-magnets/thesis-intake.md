# Thesis Intake

## Thesis Seed

- Title: Rare earth magnets and refining as a strategic bottleneck
- Date: March 15, 2026
- Source type: primary-source synthesis from IEA, DoD, and company releases
- Source URL: see `claims-audit.csv`
- Why this matters: permanent magnets built from neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium, and terbium sit inside EVs, robotics, defense systems, industrial motors, wind power, and other strategic technologies.

## Raw Claims

1. China dominates the rare-earth value chain, especially refining and magnet manufacturing.
2. Heavy rare earths used in high-performance magnets are particularly exposed and difficult to replace.
3. Finished NdFeB magnet manufacturing is the most extreme concentration point in the chain.
4. Non-China diversification is happening, but it is still too small to eliminate strategic dependence.

## Initial Dependency Chain

- End market: EVs, robotics, industrial motors, defense, wind, advanced manufacturing, AI-adjacent infrastructure
- System layer: motors, actuators, magnet-intensive industrial and defense systems
- Module layer: permanent magnets and magnet assemblies
- Component layer: sintered NdFeB magnets, heavy rare earth-enhanced magnet formulations
- Material / substrate layer: NdPr oxide/metal, dysprosium, terbium, separated rare earth products
- Geography / policy layer: China concentration, export controls, DoD-backed domestic diversification, allied mine-to-magnet projects

## Why It Might Be Mispriced

- What the market likely sees: rare earths are a strategic mineral theme with recurring geopolitical headlines
- What the market may be missing: the strongest bottleneck may be in magnet manufacturing and heavy rare earth processing rather than in mining alone
- Why the gap exists: "rare earths" is often discussed too broadly, which obscures where concentration is most extreme and where value capture may actually sit

## Expression Candidates

- Mine-to-magnet non-China buildout: MP Materials
- Allied refining / separation: Lynas
- Magnet-manufacturing proxies and defense-supported domestic magnet initiatives
- Optionality / long-vol angle: only if later market-data work shows that geopolitical and concentration risk are still underpriced

## Evidence Needed

- Primary-source proof still missing: cleaner current public share data by finished magnet manufacturer
- Capacity data still missing: exact non-China magnet-manufacturing ramp and utilization plans
- Qualification / substitution data still missing: how quickly downstream customers can swap suppliers or reformulate magnets
- Pricing / margin evidence still missing: how much state support versus free-market pricing is driving the economics of diversification

## Falsifiers

- The thesis is wrong if: non-China refining and magnet capacity scales faster than expected and meaningfully reduces dependence
- The bottleneck is weaker than expected if: end users successfully substitute away from the most constrained magnet formulations
- The value-capture layer is elsewhere if: downstream system makers or state-backed structures absorb more economics than refiners or magnet manufacturers
