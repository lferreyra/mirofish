# Case Study: Rare Earth Magnets and Refining

Date: March 15, 2026

## Objective

Run the fourth end-to-end bottleneck research workflow inside MiroFish using magnet rare earths, refining, and permanent magnet manufacturing as the pilot case.

This case study follows the same workflow as the prior pilots:

1. thesis intake
2. claims audit
3. chain reconstruction
4. chokepoint scoring
5. preliminary investment interpretation

## Thesis Seed

The starting hypothesis was that the real rare-earth bottleneck is not simply "rare earth mining." It is the concentration of:

1. magnet rare earth separation and refining
2. heavy rare earth supply used in high-performance magnets
3. sintered NdFeB permanent magnet manufacturing

This case matters because it adds a different bottleneck shape from the prior studies:

- geopolitical concentration
- processing and refining chokepoints
- heavy dependence on one country across multiple layers
- defense, robotics, EV, industrial motor, and AI-data-center relevance

## Chain Reconstruction

The useful dependency chain is:

1. magnet rare earth mining
2. separation and refining
3. heavy rare earth availability for magnet performance
4. NdFeB permanent magnet manufacturing
5. motors, robotics, EVs, defense systems, industrial equipment, and AI data-center infrastructure

The key lesson from this case is that mining alone is not the central issue. The tighter bottlenecks appear lower in the chain, especially in refining and magnet manufacturing.

## Interpretation

### What the workflow found

- Magnet manufacturing scored highest because China’s position at the finished permanent magnet layer is extreme and directly relevant to downstream industries.
- Heavy rare earth supply also scored very high because dysprosium and terbium are difficult to replace in high-performance magnets and are exposed to export controls.
- Separation and refining remain a major chokepoint because even if mining diversifies somewhat, midstream concentration remains severe.

### What this means

This case reinforces a MiroFish research rule:

`the most intuitive upstream bottleneck is not always the most binding downstream gate`

Rare earth mining matters, but the stronger constraint appears to sit at:

- refining and separation
- heavy rare earth access
- finished magnet manufacturing

That matters for investing because the public-market opportunity may be better framed around mine-to-magnet buildout and non-China industrialization than around mining exposure alone.

## Why This Is A Good Fourth Case

This case improves the framework in two ways:

- it adds a clear geopolitics-led chokepoint
- it tests whether the workflow can handle value chains where concentration is extreme across several layers at once

It also broadens the thematic scope beyond semiconductors and electric grid equipment.

## Most Important Follow-Up Questions

- Which public company has the cleanest non-China exposure at the most constrained layer?
- How quickly can U.S. and allied mine-to-magnet capacity scale in practice?
- How much of the value-capture opportunity sits in price floors, long-term offtakes, and state support rather than normal market economics?
- Do export controls on heavy rare earths and magnets remain episodic, or do they become a durable feature of the market?
