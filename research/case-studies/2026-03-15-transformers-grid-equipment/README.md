# Case Study: Transformers and Grid Equipment

Date: March 15, 2026

## Objective

Run the third end-to-end bottleneck research workflow inside MiroFish using transformers and adjacent grid equipment as the pilot case.

This case study follows the same workflow as the InP and HBM studies:

1. thesis intake
2. claims audit
3. chain reconstruction
4. chokepoint scoring
5. preliminary investment interpretation

## Thesis Seed

The starting hypothesis was that transformer and adjacent grid-equipment bottlenecks may become a durable long-duration investment theme because power-delivery infrastructure is now under pressure from:

1. AI data-center load growth
2. electrification and grid modernization
3. long manufacturing lead times
4. aging installed equipment

This is a useful test case because it is far removed from semiconductors while still fitting the same bottleneck-research model.

## Chain Reconstruction

The useful dependency chain is:

1. utility-scale power transformers
2. distribution transformers
3. high-voltage cables and related transmission equipment
4. grid interconnection and delivery systems
5. AI data centers, electrification, and broader power-demand growth

The key lesson from this case is that transformers are not just another industrial input. They sit at a slow-moving, highly physical layer of infrastructure where capacity takes years to add and replacement cycles are long.

## Interpretation

### What the workflow found

- Utility-scale power transformers scored highest because official and industry sources describe very long procurement times, hard substitution, and constrained supply.
- Distribution transformers still score high because demand is broad and rising, but they look less acute than large power transformers.
- Cables and adjacent grid equipment are also meaningfully constrained, but this layer currently looks somewhat less binding than large transformers.

### What this means

This case suggests a different style of bottleneck than the semiconductor examples:

- slower-moving
- more infrastructure-like
- more obviously physical
- less reliant on technology-cycle timing

That makes it an attractive LEAPS-style research area because the constraint is unlikely to resolve in a single quarter.

## Why This Is A Good Third Case

This case matters because it tests whether the MiroFish framework generalizes beyond AI semiconductors. The answer appears to be yes.

The same workflow still works:

- identify the physical chain
- verify supply constraints with primary sources
- rank adjacent bottlenecks
- separate structural importance from final market expression

## Most Important Follow-Up Questions

- Which listed companies have the cleanest exposure to large power transformer scarcity rather than generic electrical-equipment demand?
- How quickly can domestic and global manufacturing capacity expand?
- Will data-center load growth accelerate transformer scarcity faster than utilities and manufacturers are planning for?
- At what point do cables and other transmission components become equally binding?
